## Startup / Product Idea Validation

### Methodological Foundation

This config is anchored in Jobs to Be Done and Outcome-Driven Innovation for problem selection and unmet need quality; Sequoia’s Arc product-market fit framework for market, product, and customer fit; a16z’s work on growth metrics and distribution moats for early evidence quality and go-to-market realism; Hamilton Helmer’s *7 Powers* for whether a promising idea could ever become defensible; and Marty Cagan’s *Transformed* for product discovery discipline and evidence over opinion. I weighted problem reality and demand evidence above “idea quality,” because current practitioner thinking treats false positives on demand as the most common early-stage decision error. ([Sequoia Capital][1])

### Dimensions

#### Problem Severity — Weight: 22%

What it measures: How painful, frequent, and costly the target problem is for a clearly defined user segment.
Why it matters: Teams routinely build around interesting workflows rather than painful ones, then discover the buyer tolerates the status quo.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Multiple independent sources show the problem occurs frequently for a defined segment; active workarounds exist; users spend real time or money on the problem; at least one proxy signal exists such as job postings, search demand, communities, or budgeted tooling.
* 4 (Good): Problem is clearly documented and recurring; workarounds are visible; pain appears material, though evidence is less quantified or less segment-specific.
* 3 (Moderate): Problem is plausible and acknowledged, but frequency, cost, or intensity are only partially evidenced.
* 2 (Weak): Problem is described mostly in opinion pieces, founder intuition, or broad trend language; few concrete workarounds or behavioral signals.
* 1 (Poor): Little evidence the problem is acute; users appear satisfied with current behavior or do not meaningfully act to solve it.

#### Segment Specificity — Weight: 15%

What it measures: How sharply the initial customer and use case are defined.
Why it matters: Broad categories create false confidence because demand signals and objections vary dramatically across segments.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Initial segment is narrow and operationally clear by role, company type, trigger, and use case; evidence consistently refers to that segment.
* 4 (Good): Segment is well defined by role or customer type, with a clear use case, though some boundary conditions remain fuzzy.
* 3 (Moderate): Segment is partly specified but still aggregates materially different buyers or workflows.
* 2 (Weak): Audience is broad and generic, such as “small businesses” or “marketers,” with no clear entry wedge.
* 1 (Poor): No specific customer segment is defined beyond general market labels.

#### Demand Evidence — Weight: 18%

What it measures: The quality of observable signals that buyers will adopt or pay.
Why it matters: Founders often mistake verbal interest for demand.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Evidence includes multiple behavioral signals such as paid pilots, preorders, waitlist conversion, active budget line items, switching behavior, RFPs, or sustained organic pull.
* 4 (Good): At least one strong behavioral signal exists plus several supporting signals such as repeated inbound requests or high-intent search/community activity.
* 3 (Moderate): Evidence is mostly interviews, survey intent, or design-partner enthusiasm, with limited proof of real commitment.
* 2 (Weak): Demand is inferred from market size, trend narratives, or comparable company success rather than direct buyer behavior.
* 1 (Poor): No meaningful evidence of willingness to adopt or pay.

#### Workflow Fit & Switching Cost — Weight: 15%

What it measures: How naturally the product fits into the user’s existing workflow and whether adoption friction is manageable.
Why it matters: Many good ideas fail because they require too much behavior change relative to the perceived gain.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Product slots into an existing high-frequency workflow; implementation burden is low; buyer can trial without major process redesign; existing alternatives create visible frustration.
* 4 (Good): Workflow fit is credible and value is clear, but adoption requires moderate setup, retraining, or integration.
* 3 (Moderate): Value proposition is understandable, but switching requires notable habit change or process adjustment.
* 2 (Weak): Product requires users to learn a new workflow, maintain duplicate systems, or reorganize ownership before value appears.
* 1 (Poor): Adoption would require major behavior change with weak immediate payoff.

#### Monetization Logic — Weight: 12%

What it measures: Whether there is a credible path from use to revenue with sane economics.
Why it matters: Teams often validate usefulness without validating a buyer, budget, or pricing mechanism.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Clear payer exists; pricing basis matches value metric; plausible gross margin and payback logic can be inferred from public benchmarks or comparable tools.
* 4 (Good): Buyer and pricing motion are mostly clear, though some assumptions on budget source or sales cost remain untested.
* 3 (Moderate): Monetization is plausible but depends on uncertain packaging, unclear buyer ownership, or thin benchmark support.
* 2 (Weak): Product seems useful, but payer, contract size, or economic model is vague.
* 1 (Poor): No credible monetization path beyond speculative future upsell or scale.

#### Distribution Advantage — Weight: 10%

What it measures: Whether the team has a credible way to reach the first customers efficiently.
Why it matters: Early products often die from customer acquisition friction rather than product quality.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): There is a specific repeatable wedge such as founder access, embedded distribution, ecosystem partner, community authority, or bottom-up viral loop with evidence from analogs.
* 4 (Good): One realistic acquisition channel stands out and appears cost-effective for the target segment.
* 3 (Moderate): Potential channels are identifiable, but none clearly look advantaged or efficient.
* 2 (Weak): Go-to-market assumes crowded paid acquisition, broad outbound, or “content” without evidence of channel fit.
* 1 (Poor): No believable customer acquisition motion is visible.

#### Defensibility Potential — Weight: 8%

What it measures: Whether the idea could plausibly develop durable advantage if it works.
Why it matters: Some products solve real problems but remain too easy to copy to justify investment.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Clear path exists to one or more durable powers such as switching costs, scale economies, workflow embedding, network effects, cornered resources, or process power.
* 4 (Good): Early signs of defensibility exist, though compounding mechanisms are not yet proven.
* 3 (Moderate): Product may gain temporary advantage through execution or speed, but durable moat is uncertain.
* 2 (Weak): Advantage appears mostly feature-based and easily replicable by better-distributed incumbents.
* 1 (Poor): No credible source of durable differentiation is visible.

### Weight Rationale

Problem Severity gets the highest weight because modern product and venture practice still treats acute unmet need as the hardest thing to fake and the most important filter for early opportunities. Demand Evidence is next because Sequoia- and a16z-style thinking puts heavy emphasis on observable pull over narrative. Segment Specificity and Workflow Fit matter more than defensibility at this stage because early failures usually come from weak wedge definition or adoption friction before moat ever matters. Monetization and Distribution are meaningful but secondary to proving the problem is worth solving; Defensibility is deliberately lighter because it is often emergent early, though still worth tracking. ([Sequoia Capital][1])

### RelatedDiscovery: Yes

Idea validation often surfaces adjacent segments, adjacent jobs, and sharper entry wedges. Those are usually valuable outputs rather than noise.

### Most Commonly Misjudged Dimensions

Demand Evidence: teams overweight interview enthusiasm and underweight behavioral proof.
Distribution Advantage: founders often assume “good products spread” and under-model the cost of reaching the first repeatable customers.

## Market Entry Analysis

### Methodological Foundation

This config is grounded in McKinsey’s current “where to play” and market-specific competitive-advantage work, especially its emphasis that advantage is context-specific at the intersection of offering, geography, and customer; BCG’s strategy and sector work on uneven market conditions and local fit; Bain’s strategic-fit logic; and classic strategy books that remain widely used in practice, especially Rumelt on diagnosis and coherent action. The result is not a generic “country attractiveness” template: it is a right-to-win test for a specific offer in a specific market. ([McKinsey & Company][2])

### Dimensions

#### Market Attractiveness — Weight: 20%

What it measures: The size, growth, profit pool, and structural momentum of the target market.
Why it matters: Teams often enter large but unattractive markets with weak margins or slowing demand.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Credible sources show large and growing demand, healthy margins or spend intensity, and tailwinds that support multi-year entry economics.
* 4 (Good): Market is clearly attractive on size and growth, though profit pool or demand durability is less certain.
* 3 (Moderate): Market has either scale or growth, but not both clearly; attractiveness depends on assumptions.
* 2 (Weak): Market is small, slowing, margin-thin, or structurally difficult despite headline size.
* 1 (Poor): Market is unattractive on both scale and economics.

#### Local Need Fit — Weight: 18%

What it measures: How well the offer matches local customer needs, buying criteria, and usage conditions.
Why it matters: Companies over-extrapolate from success in one market to another.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Local customer needs, purchasing criteria, and product requirements are well evidenced and closely match the proposed offer with minimal adaptation.
* 4 (Good): Core value proposition appears to translate, but some feature, pricing, or service localization is needed.
* 3 (Moderate): Need fit is plausible but partially inferred from adjacent markets or broad similarities.
* 2 (Weak): Significant adaptation appears necessary across product, pricing, or support.
* 1 (Poor): The offer does not clearly solve a locally valued problem.

#### Competitive Right-to-Win — Weight: 18%

What it measures: Whether the entrant has a real basis to win against incumbents and substitutes.
Why it matters: Market entry fails when firms choose attractive markets where they lack a nontrivial advantage.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Entrant has a differentiated asset or model that local competitors would struggle to match, and that difference matters to buyers.
* 4 (Good): Entrant has a credible edge on product, cost, channel, brand, or partner access, though incumbent response remains possible.
* 3 (Moderate): Entrant could compete, but advantage is narrow or not yet proven locally.
* 2 (Weak): Entry case relies mostly on being “better” without clear buyer-visible differentiation.
* 1 (Poor): No clear right-to-win versus local incumbents or substitutes.

#### Route-to-Market Feasibility — Weight: 15%

What it measures: The practicality of acquiring customers and serving them in the target market.
Why it matters: Strong products fail in new markets because channel, trust, and local sales motion are wrong.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Clear acquisition path exists through proven channels, local partners, existing accounts, or embedded distribution with evidence of feasibility.
* 4 (Good): Channel strategy is credible and resourced, though conversion assumptions are partly untested.
* 3 (Moderate): Several channels are possible, but none clearly stand out as efficient.
* 2 (Weak): Go-to-market depends on expensive awareness building or weak local access.
* 1 (Poor): No realistic distribution or sales motion is identified.

#### Regulatory & Operating Friction — Weight: 14%

What it measures: The burden from regulation, compliance, localization, and execution complexity.
Why it matters: Entry cases often underprice operating friction and time to readiness.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Regulatory requirements are clear and manageable; operating model can support local needs without major redesign.
* 4 (Good): Some compliance or localization work is needed, but effort is bounded and well understood.
* 3 (Moderate): Material friction exists, but can likely be managed with investment.
* 2 (Weak): Regulatory, tax, labor, or service complexity materially threatens timing or margins.
* 1 (Poor): Friction is severe enough to undermine the entry case.

#### Entry Economics — Weight: 15%

What it measures: Expected payback, gross margin, CAC-to-LTV logic, and investment required to establish presence.
Why it matters: Attractive markets still destroy value when entry costs and ramp time are underestimated.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Entry investment, ramp assumptions, and unit economics support a credible payback case under conservative scenarios.
* 4 (Good): Economics look favorable, though one or two assumptions remain sensitivity points.
* 3 (Moderate): Economics are plausible but depend on optimistic adoption, pricing, or efficiency assumptions.
* 2 (Weak): Payback appears long or fragile; economics worsen materially under moderate downside cases.
* 1 (Poor): Entry is unlikely to create value on realistic assumptions.

### Weight Rationale

Market Attractiveness leads because bad markets are hard to out-execute. Local Need Fit and Competitive Right-to-Win are nearly as heavy because McKinsey’s current work explicitly argues advantage is market-specific, not portable in the abstract. Route-to-Market and Entry Economics matter next because many entry failures are commercial rather than strategic. Regulatory & Operating Friction is slightly lower only because its importance varies by industry, but it should spike for fintech, health, defense, and other regulated categories. ([McKinsey & Company][3])

### RelatedDiscovery: Yes

Market-entry work often reveals better starting segments, partner-led entry paths, and lower-friction geographies that should be surfaced.

### Most Commonly Misjudged Dimensions

Local Need Fit: teams assume the “same problem” means the same product and pricing will work.
Route-to-Market Feasibility: especially in B2B, leaders underestimate trust, local relationships, and channel realities.

## Competitive Landscape

### Methodological Foundation

This config draws on Helmer’s durable-advantage lens, McKinsey’s granular competitive-advantage approach, and practitioner venture thinking from a16z and Sequoia that focuses less on feature comparison and more on what actually drives customer choice, market power, and white space. I excluded generic checklist competitor matrices as the scoring backbone; the point here is to understand the structure of advantage, not just list vendors. ([Andreessen Horowitz][4])

### Dimensions

#### Category Structure — Weight: 18%

What it measures: Whether the market is fragmented, concentrated, bundled, rapidly consolidating, or still unsettled.
Why it matters: Misreading category structure leads to bad assumptions about pricing power and entry difficulty.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Landscape structure is clearly mapped with identifiable leaders, challengers, substitutes, and basis of competition.
* 4 (Good): Major players and structure are clear, though some adjacent substitutes or emerging entrants remain less mapped.
* 3 (Moderate): Primary competitors are known, but category boundaries and substitute set are incomplete.
* 2 (Weak): Analysis focuses on obvious named players only.
* 1 (Poor): No coherent view of the category or substitute landscape.

#### Customer Choice Drivers — Weight: 18%

What it measures: The attributes that actually determine customer selection and retention.
Why it matters: Teams often benchmark flashy features instead of the factors buyers truly value.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Multiple sources show clear buyer decision criteria and trade-offs, with evidence from reviews, win-loss signals, case studies, or practitioner commentary.
* 4 (Good): Decision criteria are mostly clear, though some segments may value different things.
* 3 (Moderate): Several plausible criteria are identified, but relative importance is not well evidenced.
* 2 (Weak): Criteria are inferred from vendor messaging more than customer evidence.
* 1 (Poor): No clear view of what drives customer choice.

#### Competitor Advantage Quality — Weight: 18%

What it measures: Whether leading competitors have real durable advantages or only temporary feature leads.
Why it matters: Underestimating incumbent power is a classic strategy error.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Leading competitors show durable powers such as switching costs, ecosystem control, scale economics, proprietary distribution, network effects, or embedded workflow ownership.
* 4 (Good): Several competitors have meaningful but not impregnable advantages.
* 3 (Moderate): Advantages exist but look mixed, local, or vulnerable to change.
* 2 (Weak): Claimed moats appear mostly branding or feature breadth without clear compounding mechanisms.
* 1 (Poor): No strong competitor advantage is evident.

#### White-Space Quality — Weight: 16%

What it measures: Whether meaningful unmet needs or underserved segments remain open.
Why it matters: Not all gaps are worth pursuing; many are too small or structurally unattractive.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Specific unmet needs or segments are repeatedly visible, with evidence that existing tools underperform and buyers care.
* 4 (Good): Plausible gaps exist, though evidence on monetization or segment size is thinner.
* 3 (Moderate): Some whitespace is visible, but could simply reflect low demand.
* 2 (Weak): Gaps look cosmetic, tiny, or already being addressed by multiple entrants.
* 1 (Poor): No credible whitespace is visible.

#### Pace of Change — Weight: 12%

What it measures: How quickly the landscape is shifting through technology, regulation, bundling, or buyer behavior.
Why it matters: Static snapshots become misleading fast in fast-moving categories.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Clear evidence shows rapid shifts in product architecture, pricing, distribution, or buyer expectations; today’s leaders may not map cleanly to tomorrow’s.
* 4 (Good): Category is evolving materially, though main competitive frame still holds.
* 3 (Moderate): Change is present but not enough to overturn current structure soon.
* 2 (Weak): Category is relatively stable and slow moving.
* 1 (Poor): No meaningful competitive change is visible.

#### Relative Positioning Risk — Weight: 18%

What it measures: How exposed the focal company or idea is relative to current competitors.
Why it matters: A landscape map is useless if it does not translate into specific strategic risk.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Focal position is clear, differentiated, and defensible against the most relevant competitors and substitutes.
* 4 (Good): Position is credible, though one or two competitor responses could weaken it.
* 3 (Moderate): Position is plausible but overlaps substantially with existing offerings.
* 2 (Weak): Focal offer is hard to distinguish or easy for incumbents to neutralize.
* 1 (Poor): Focal offer is undifferentiated and strategically exposed.

### Weight Rationale

This research type weights customer choice, competitor advantage, and relative positioning above raw market mapping because practitioners use competitor work to answer “how do we win or avoid losing,” not “who exists.” White space matters, but only after understanding what customers value and what incumbents can defend. Pace of Change is lower because it is a modifier rather than the central decision variable in most landscape work. ([McKinsey & Company][3])

### RelatedDiscovery: No

The main job here is clarity, not ideation. Adjacent ideas usually distract from the purpose of a competitive readout.

### Most Commonly Misjudged Dimensions

Customer Choice Drivers: teams substitute analyst opinions and feature tables for actual buyer priorities.
Competitor Advantage Quality: people often mistake “incumbent looks outdated” for “incumbent is weak.”

## Build vs. Buy / Technology Decision

### Methodological Foundation

This config uses BCG’s current buy-and-build logic, which explicitly favors hybrid approaches when companies need both speed and differentiation; McKinsey’s recent framing that build-versus-buy should be evaluated in light of competitive advantage and strategic flexibility; Bain’s execution-first view that capabilities should be sequenced to deliver value while building lasting internal strengths; and SVPG’s product-operating-model view that capabilities matter only when they materially affect product outcomes. This is why the config gives high weight to strategic criticality and time-to-value instead of treating the choice as a pure cost comparison. ([BCG Global][5])

### Dimensions

#### Strategic Criticality — Weight: 22%

What it measures: Whether the capability is core to competitive advantage or merely enabling infrastructure.
Why it matters: Firms overspend building commodity capabilities and underspend owning the few things that should differentiate them.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Capability directly shapes customer value, margin structure, control of data/workflow, or long-term moat.
* 4 (Good): Capability is important to strategic performance, though not the sole differentiator.
* 3 (Moderate): Capability matters operationally but does not obviously define competitive advantage.
* 2 (Weak): Capability is mostly supporting infrastructure.
* 1 (Poor): Capability is commodity and not strategically distinctive.

#### Time-to-Value — Weight: 18%

What it measures: How quickly the organization can capture usable value under each path.
Why it matters: Build decisions often look elegant but miss the market window.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): One path clearly delivers production value quickly with limited dependency risk and realistic implementation timing.
* 4 (Good): Value can be achieved within an acceptable window, though some dependencies remain.
* 3 (Moderate): Time-to-value is acceptable but uncertain.
* 2 (Weak): Delivery timing is long or heavily contingent on prerequisites.
* 1 (Poor): Value is unlikely to arrive in time to matter.

#### Differentiation Need — Weight: 18%

What it measures: How much custom behavior, proprietary workflow, or unique IP is required.
Why it matters: Buying the wrong thing can flatten differentiation; building the wrong thing wastes resources.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Success requires substantial customization, proprietary logic, or close fit to distinctive workflows.
* 4 (Good): Some important differentiation is needed, but a commercial base with custom layers could work.
* 3 (Moderate): Moderate tailoring is useful but not decisive.
* 2 (Weak): Standard solutions likely satisfy most needs.
* 1 (Poor): Little to no differentiation is required.

#### Integration & Change Burden — Weight: 16%

What it measures: The technical, process, and organizational effort needed to deploy and sustain the solution.
Why it matters: Hidden integration and adoption costs often dominate headline licensing or build costs.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Integration path is clear; required process change is manageable; dependencies are bounded.
* 4 (Good): Some complexity exists, but architecture and ownership are mostly understood.
* 3 (Moderate): Integration is feasible but touches several systems or teams.
* 2 (Weak): Significant legacy, data, or process complexity threatens execution.
* 1 (Poor): Integration and change burden likely swamp expected value.

#### Vendor / Lock-In Risk — Weight: 14%

What it measures: Exposure to vendor dependency, roadmap misalignment, switching cost, and commercial leverage.
Why it matters: “Buy” can solve near-term speed while creating strategic rigidity.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Vendor risk is low, with strong interoperability, contract clarity, and acceptable switching paths.
* 4 (Good): Some dependency exists, but can be managed contractually or architecturally.
* 3 (Moderate): Lock-in risk is material but tolerable relative to value.
* 2 (Weak): Vendor controls critical data, workflow, or economics in ways that could constrain the business.
* 1 (Poor): Dependency risk is strategically unacceptable.

#### Internal Capability Readiness — Weight: 12%

What it measures: Whether the organization can successfully build, integrate, and evolve the capability.
Why it matters: Many build decisions are really aspiration decisions unsupported by talent and operating model.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Team has relevant talent, product ownership, architecture, and delivery track record.
* 4 (Good): Capability gaps are modest and realistically fillable.
* 3 (Moderate): Build path is possible but depends on hiring, new governance, or major learning.
* 2 (Weak): Organization lacks key talent or product/engineering maturity.
* 1 (Poor): Internal readiness is far below what the build path requires.

### Weight Rationale

Strategic Criticality carries the most weight because current consulting guidance increasingly ties build-versus-buy to advantage ownership, not just procurement efficiency. Time-to-Value is next because BCG’s buy-and-build logic explicitly favors speed where proven solutions exist. Differentiation Need and Integration & Change Burden sit in the middle because they decide whether a hybrid architecture is sensible. Vendor Risk matters, but only in relation to the value being outsourced. Internal Capability Readiness is lighter only because weak readiness can sometimes be bought or partnered around, though it remains a frequent execution killer. ([BCG Global][5])

### RelatedDiscovery: Yes

This research often reveals hybrid options, phased ownership models, and adjacent capabilities worth building later.

### Most Commonly Misjudged Dimensions

Integration & Change Burden: buyers compare software features and ignore process change.
Internal Capability Readiness: leaders often assume an engineering team can build anything strategic if given enough time.

## Investment / M&A Screening

### Methodological Foundation

This config combines current M&A practice from McKinsey, BCG, and Bain with venture-style quality filters from Sequoia and a16z. The consulting side contributes strategic fit, synergy logic, diligence beyond the standard legal-financial checklist, and repeated emphasis on experienced acquirers, value creation, and deeper diligence. The venture side contributes market quality, growth quality, and defensibility. I deliberately weight quality of advantage and value-creation logic above raw market excitement because practitioners are trying to avoid expensive false positives, not just find “interesting” assets. ([McKinsey & Company][6])

### Dimensions

#### Strategic Fit — Weight: 20%

What it measures: How well the target advances the acquirer’s or investor’s explicit strategy.
Why it matters: Deals fail when the asset is attractive in isolation but not useful in portfolio context.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Target clearly strengthens a named strategic priority such as capability, market access, product line, or defensible adjacency.
* 4 (Good): Fit is credible and specific, though multiple strategic narratives remain possible.
* 3 (Moderate): Fit exists, but is broad or partly opportunistic.
* 2 (Weak): Strategic rationale is vague, mostly financial, or post hoc.
* 1 (Poor): No coherent strategic fit is visible.

#### Market Quality — Weight: 15%

What it measures: The attractiveness of the market or category the target serves.
Why it matters: Even good assets struggle in structurally bad markets.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Market shows strong demand, healthy economics, and favorable medium-term structure.
* 4 (Good): Market quality is positive overall, with some manageable risks.
* 3 (Moderate): Market is acceptable but mixed on growth, margins, or stability.
* 2 (Weak): Market quality is weak or deteriorating.
* 1 (Poor): Target is exposed to structurally unattractive markets.

#### Quality of Advantage — Weight: 18%

What it measures: Whether the target has durable competitive advantage rather than temporary momentum.
Why it matters: Buyers and investors overpay for growth that is not protected.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Target has clear durable powers visible in customer behavior, economics, or market structure.
* 4 (Good): Advantage is real but not fully hardened.
* 3 (Moderate): Target has strengths, but durability is uncertain.
* 2 (Weak): Performance appears driven by execution bursts, temporary market conditions, or marketing.
* 1 (Poor): No durable advantage is evident.

#### Value-Creation Levers — Weight: 17%

What it measures: The number, quality, and plausibility of specific levers that can increase value after the deal.
Why it matters: Deals disappoint when value creation is vague or relies on one fragile synergy story.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Multiple concrete levers exist, such as cross-sell, pricing, channel leverage, cost takeout, product integration, or capability transfer, each with visible evidence.
* 4 (Good): At least one major and one secondary lever look credible.
* 3 (Moderate): Value creation is plausible but concentrated in a narrow set of assumptions.
* 2 (Weak): Levers are generic or difficult to operationalize.
* 1 (Poor): No credible post-deal value creation case is visible.

#### Execution & Integration Risk — Weight: 15%

What it measures: The difficulty of integrating the asset and realizing value.
Why it matters: Many deals are strategically sound and still fail in execution.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Integration scope is clear; talent, systems, culture, and regulatory risks appear manageable.
* 4 (Good): Some integration risk exists, but no obvious deal-breaker is visible.
* 3 (Moderate): Risks are meaningful and require disciplined execution.
* 2 (Weak): Integration is likely to be slow, politically difficult, or operationally disruptive.
* 1 (Poor): Realization risk is severe enough to threaten the thesis.

#### Deal Economics — Weight: 15%

What it measures: Whether price, capital needs, and downside protection support an attractive risk-adjusted return.
Why it matters: Good assets become bad deals at the wrong price.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Valuation is supported by public comps or deal logic, downside is bounded, and return case does not rely on heroic assumptions.
* 4 (Good): Economics are reasonable, though one or two assumptions do real work.
* 3 (Moderate): Deal could work, but return case is sensitive to growth, synergy, or exit assumptions.
* 2 (Weak): Price or capital requirements leave little room for execution misses.
* 1 (Poor): Economics are unattractive on realistic assumptions.

### Weight Rationale

Strategic Fit comes first because current M&A guidance repeatedly stresses that value comes from clear strategic purpose, not opportunism. Quality of Advantage and Value-Creation Levers are next because they determine whether the target can sustain value and whether the acquirer can unlock more of it. Market Quality, Execution Risk, and Deal Economics matter materially, but I kept them slightly lower because many practitioners will tolerate market or integration complexity if fit and value creation are unusually strong. ([McKinsey & Company][6])

### RelatedDiscovery: Yes

Screening often surfaces adjacent targets, tuck-ins, partnership substitutes, and alternative routes to the same strategic objective.

### Most Commonly Misjudged Dimensions

Value-Creation Levers: teams overstate generic synergies and under-specify the operating work required.
Execution & Integration Risk: buyers systematically underrate systems, culture, and ownership friction.

## Product Expansion (new feature, adjacent segment, new geography)

### Methodological Foundation

This config is based on McKinsey’s current work on growing within and beyond the core, Bain’s long-running but still practitioner-relevant adjacency and “beyond the core” logic, Sequoia’s product-market-fit framing, and a16z’s growth thinking around expansion metrics and land-and-expand behavior. I treat expansion as a transfer test: can the company carry real advantage from the core into a new feature, segment, or geography without destroying focus or economics? ([McKinsey & Company][7])

### Dimensions

#### Core Strength Transferability — Weight: 22%

What it measures: Whether the company’s existing advantage actually carries into the expansion.
Why it matters: Companies confuse adjacent demand with adjacent right-to-win.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Existing assets such as brand, data, distribution, customer relationships, workflow position, or capabilities clearly transfer into the new move.
* 4 (Good): Several core strengths transfer, though some must be adapted.
* 3 (Moderate): Transferability is plausible but limited.
* 2 (Weak): Expansion relies mostly on building new capabilities from scratch.
* 1 (Poor): Little of the current advantage meaningfully transfers.

#### Adjacency Attractiveness — Weight: 18%

What it measures: The size, growth, and economic quality of the new feature area, segment, or geography.
Why it matters: Firms often expand into adjacent spaces that look logical but are not attractive enough.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Adjacency has meaningful size or strategic value, favorable growth, and credible economics.
* 4 (Good): Attractive overall, though one dimension such as margin or scale is less compelling.
* 3 (Moderate): Adjacency is viable but not clearly high quality.
* 2 (Weak): Opportunity is niche, low-margin, or structurally difficult.
* 1 (Poor): Adjacency is unattractive despite seeming nearby.

#### Customer Pull & Cross-Sell Evidence — Weight: 16%

What it measures: Whether existing or target customers are actually asking for the expansion.
Why it matters: Internal adjacency logic often substitutes for market demand.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Repeated customer requests, expansion usage patterns, upsell behavior, or win-loss evidence show real pull.
* 4 (Good): Strong anecdotal and some behavioral evidence support demand.
* 3 (Moderate): Demand is plausible but only partially evidenced.
* 2 (Weak): Expansion is justified mainly by strategy narratives rather than customer behavior.
* 1 (Poor): Little evidence customers want the expansion.

#### Channel & Operational Readiness — Weight: 14%

What it measures: Whether the organization can sell, deliver, and support the expansion without major breakdowns.
Why it matters: Expansion often fails in execution long before strategy is tested.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Sales motion, onboarding, support, and operational ownership are clear and largely reusable from the core.
* 4 (Good): Some changes are needed, but operational path is manageable.
* 3 (Moderate): Readiness is mixed and depends on new hires, partners, or process redesign.
* 2 (Weak): Expansion requires substantially new channel or operating capabilities.
* 1 (Poor): Organization is not operationally ready.

#### Cannibalization & Complexity Risk — Weight: 14%

What it measures: The risk that expansion adds confusion, cost, or internal conflict faster than value.
Why it matters: Expansion can weaken the core through product sprawl and organizational drag.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Limited cannibalization; complexity is controlled; governance and scope are clear.
* 4 (Good): Some overlap or complexity exists but appears manageable.
* 3 (Moderate): Trade-offs are real and require disciplined prioritization.
* 2 (Weak): Expansion risks confusing positioning, bloating roadmap, or straining teams.
* 1 (Poor): Complexity or cannibalization likely outweighs upside.

#### Economic Upside — Weight: 16%

What it measures: Whether the expansion can create meaningful incremental revenue, margin, or strategic leverage.
Why it matters: Many expansions consume focus without moving company economics.
Scoring rubric:

* 5 (Strong): Expansion can materially improve revenue, retention, wallet share, margin, or strategic control on realistic assumptions.
* 4 (Good): Economic upside is clearly positive, though not transformative.
* 3 (Moderate): Upside exists but is modest or scenario-sensitive.
* 2 (Weak): Incremental upside is limited relative to required investment.
* 1 (Poor): Expansion has weak economic payoff.

### Weight Rationale

Core Strength Transferability gets the highest weight because the best expansion moves reuse an existing edge rather than start over. Adjacency Attractiveness follows because good execution in a poor adjacency still creates mediocre outcomes. Customer Pull and Economic Upside matter next: modern expansion logic is less about abstract adjacency and more about real demand plus material payoff. Channel Readiness and Complexity Risk are slightly lighter, but they should be increased for physical operations, regulated products, or multi-country rollouts. ([McKinsey & Company][7])

### Most Commonly Misjudged Dimensions

Core Strength Transferability: leaders assume existing brand or product success transfers farther than it actually does.
Cannibalization & Complexity Risk: teams underprice the organizational cost of adding adjacent bets.

A few implementation notes for your config system:

The least standardized dimensions here are Defensibility Potential in very early idea validation, Relative Positioning Risk for pre-launch concepts, and Cultural/Integration elements inside M&A if you later split those out into a separate dimension. Those are still useful, but they usually need stronger evidence thresholds than the more established market, demand, and economic dimensions. ([McKinsey & Company][6])

The main design principle across all six configs is that score weight follows decision irreversibility. Dimensions that catch expensive false positives, such as problem severity, local fit, strategic fit, and core-strength transferability, carry more weight than dimensions that mostly refine execution after the decision is already directionally sound.
